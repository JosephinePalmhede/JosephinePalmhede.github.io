Demonstration of the Data Science Pipleine Using Twitch Broadcaster Analytics¶

Author: Eva Josephine Palmhede

Introduction¶

Twitch.tv is an online platform that hosts millions of broadcasters who stream content to viewers every day. Many of these broadcasters create content on Twitch for a living; they get money through ad revenue, donations, and Twitch's subscription service. All three of these rely on the broadcaster maintaining a high view count during their streams. If there are no viewers, there are no people to watch adds, dontate, or subscribe.

For Twitch streamers, analytics are everything. When income is directly reliant on your popularity and ability to connect with an audience, knowing the patterns of viewership and exposure is important.

Based on my initial knowledge of the relationship between these stats, I have two questions I would like to explore. After the exploratory data analysis I will revisit them and make any edits I think are appropriate before I start testing any hypothesis.

Questions: Does a high average view count translate to a higher ratio of new viewers and followers? Does a high follower count translate to a high average view count?

Let's take some time to understand this data so that we can think about how the stats might interact. Keep in mind that these stats are collected over one year and not over the channel's lifetime.

Watch time on twitch means the number of minutes summed over all viewers that people have watched a stream. Stream time is the total number of minutes a channel has spent live over the past year. Peak viewers and average viewers are the highest number of viewers you've had and the number of viewers you average over all streams, respectively. Views gained are the number of new viewers a stream has gotten. Followers and viewers gained are the number of followers or viewers gained by a streamer in the past year.

The last three columns, partnered, mature, and language, are a little different. Partnered on twitch means that viewers are able to subscribe to the channel and the streamer gets a paycheck from twitch based on the number of subscribers they have. Mature means the stream is marked for an audience that is 18+. Language is the primary language the streamer speaks in and the chat speaks in.

This table is very interesting to us. We can see the correlation between followers and average view count as well as correlation between average view count and new views & follows. This gives us information on whether or not we can expect our hypothesis will hold.

On twitch's website, streams are ordered by view count. So streams with a high number of viewers are more discoverable. We can see that there is a high correlation between followers and followers gained, which makes sense. There is also a high correlation between followers and watch time. What we care about for our hypothesis, though, are the correlations between followers and average view count as well as correlation between average view count and new follows & new viewers. We can see that the first two are around .42 while average view count and new viewers is much lower at .27. That last stat is interesting because our hypothesis says that we expect a high correlation there.

Exploratory Data Analysis¶

In this section I am first going to create a heatmap as a visual for the correlation between broadcaster's statistics. Then I will create three plots to explore my hypotheses. The first will show compare a stream's average viewers to the broadcaster's average followers. The idea behind this one is that more followers should equate to more viewers, since more people are being notified when the broadcaster is live. The second will compare a stream's average viewers versus the broadcaster's followers gained. In theory, more viewers should lead to more new people clicking the follow button on the stream. The third and last plot will compare a stream's average viewers versus its average views gained. More viewers should place the stream higher on the discover list, leading to more new people finding the stream.

Next I will fit a linear regression to these plots and see how it turns out. But before that I will completely form my hypotheses and null hypotheses. The questions I started with were: Does a high follower count translate to a high average view count? Does a high average view count translate to a higher ratio of new viewers and followers?

My first hypothesis is that a high follower count translates to a high average view count. And I have the same null hypothesis that there is no relationship between the two statistics.

My second hypothesis is that a high average view count translates to a higher ratio of new viewers and followers. My null hypotheis is that there is no relationship between the two statistics.

Communication of Insights Attained¶

A review of the data science process:

  1. Scrape the data
  2. Format and tidy the data
  3. Explore the data and develop a hypothesis
  4. Test your hypothesis
  5. Draw conclusions

Now that we have gone through the steps of the datascience pipeline, I will conclude with a summary of what we analyzed and learned.

My two hypotheses were that a high follower count translates to a high average view count and my second hypothesis was that a high average view count translates to a higher ratio of new viewers and followers. What I learned was that it is hard to make conclusions like that about the top 100 streamers on twitch, perhaps because they are too large for general trends like that to affect them. What I was able to say was that a low number of followers correlates to a lower average number of viewers. I also concluded that a high average view count will translate to both a higher number of followers gained and a higher number of viewers gained. I attribute this to the method twitch uses to push streams, putting the ones with the most views at the top.
